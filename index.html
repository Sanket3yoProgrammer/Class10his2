1869

Gandhi's Early Life and Return to India

October 2, 1869: Mahatma Gandhi was born in Porbandar, Gujarat.

1888: Gandhi traveled to London to study law.

1893: Gandhi went to South Africa to work as a lawyer. Satyagraha was developed here after he faced racial discrimination. → Meaning: Satyagraha is a form of non-violent resistance.

1915

Gandhi's Return to India and Initial Satyagrahas

January 9, 1915: Gandhi returned to India from South Africa.

1917: Champaran Satyagraha (Bihar). → Cause: Indigo farmers were forced to grow indigo under the oppressive tinkathia system, where they had to cultivate indigo on 3/20th of their land and sell it at prices fixed by European planters. This led to severe exploitation and financial hardships for the farmers.

1918: Ahmedabad Mill Strike - Gandhi led a successful strike for better wages. → Cause: Workers in the textile mills of Ahmedabad were demanding a 50% wage increase due to inflation and the increased cost of living during World War I. Mill owners were unwilling to agree to these demands, leading to the strike.

1918: Kheda Satyagraha (Gujarat) - Gandhi led peasants in a tax revolt. → Cause: Due to crop failures and a plague epidemic, the peasants of Kheda were unable to pay the high taxes demanded by the British government. Despite their inability to pay, the government refused to suspend the tax collection, prompting the revolt.

1919

The Khilafat Movement and Rowlatt Satyagraha

1919: Khilafat Movement - Aimed to protect the position of the Caliph. → The Khilafat Movement was also an effort to unite Hindus and Muslims in the freedom struggle.

1919: Rowlatt Act and Satyagraha - Allowed the British to imprison anyone without trial for two years.

April 13, 1919: Jallianwala Bagh Massacre - Hundreds were killed by General Dyer's orders. → This event fueled nationalistic fervor and anger against British rule.

1920

Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-1922)

September 1920: Non-Cooperation Movement launched due to the Jallianwala Bagh Massacre, Rowlatt Act, and Khilafat issue.

Actions: Boycott of British institutions and promotion of swadeshi goods. → Swadeshi means promoting indigenous products and boycotting foreign goods.

February 1922: Chauri Chaura Incident - A mob set a police station on fire, leading to Gandhi calling off the movement.

1927-1929

Post-Non-Cooperation Movements

1927: Simon Commission protests due to lack of Indian representation.

1929: Purna Swaraj Declaration - Congress declares Complete Independence as its goal.

1930-1934

The Civil Disobedience Movement (1930-1934)

March 12-April 6, 1930: Dandi March - Gandhi's march to break the salt law. → This event marked the start of the Civil Disobedience Movement.

1931: Gandhi-Irwin Pact - Gandhi agreed to suspend the movement in exchange for talks.

1932: Communal Award and Poona Pact - Compromise between Gandhi and Ambedkar on separate electorates for Dalits.

1942-1947

Quit India Movement and Demand for Pakistan

August 8, 1942: Quit India Movement - Gandhi's call for "Do or Die". → A mass movement demanding an end to British rule in India.

1940-1947: Demand for Pakistan - Led to the partition of India and Pakistan in 1947. → The partition resulted in widespread violence and displacement.

1947

Legacy of the Freedom Struggle

1947: India gained independence on August 15, 1947. The freedom struggle left a lasting legacy on the nation and the world.

National Symbols: The tricolor flag, Bharat Mata, and the slogan Vande Mataram became symbols of unity and resistance. → These symbols inspired the masses and united diverse communities in the fight for independence.

Gandhi's Legacy: His philosophy of non-violence and satyagraha influenced civil rights movements across the world.
